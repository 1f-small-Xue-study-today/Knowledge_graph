## Week 1 — Polynomial Trajectory Optimization

This week develops a rigorous understanding of minimum-snap polynomial trajectories and their quadratic‑programming (QP) formulation.  A key result is that to minimize the \(r\)-th derivative of a trajectory you need a polynomial of order at least \(r+1\) (for minimum snap, \(r=4\) so order ≥ 5; the VNAV notes use a 9th‑order polynomial).  The cost functional can be written as \(J=\int_0^T [P^{(r)}(t)]^2\,dt\), subject to boundary conditions, and it can be reformulated as a QP problem \(\min p^T Q p\) with linear equality constraints \(A p = b\).  You will derive these forms and implement solvers.

### Day 1 (Mon Mar 9) – Minimum‑Snap Basics

- Read section 10.1 of MIT's trajectory‑optimisation notes to understand why minimizing the \(r\)-th derivative requires a polynomial of order at least \(r+1\).
- Derive the cost functional for minimum‑snap in 1D:
  
  \[
  J = \int_0^T [x^{(4)}(t)]^2\,dt,
  \]
  
  with position and velocity boundary conditions at \(t=0\) and \(t=T\).
- Write the Euler–Lagrange equations and derive the general solution for a 7th‑order polynomial; show why higher‑order polynomials allow satisfying boundary conditions.
- Implement a **Python** script that solves for the 1D polynomial coefficients using symbolic integration or the QP formulation \(\min p^T Q p\).
- Plot the resulting trajectory and its derivatives; discuss the impact of increasing polynomial order.

### Day 2 (Tue Mar 10) – Piecewise Polynomials & QP Formulation

- Extend the derivation to piecewise polynomials passing through multiple waypoints.  Define a vector of coefficients \(p\) for each segment and assemble continuity constraints for position, velocity, acceleration and jerk at the knots.
- Derive the global cost \(J = p^T Q p\) by integrating the squared snap for each segment and assembling the block‑diagonal matrix \(Q\).
- Formulate the linear equality constraints \(A p = b\) to enforce boundary conditions and continuity.
- Implement a solver that uses a QP library (e.g. `cvxpy`) to find the coefficients.
- Test the solver on a trajectory through three waypoints; compare the solution with a hand‑derived 7th‑order polynomial.

### Day 3 (Wed Mar 11) – 3D Minimum‑Snap Trajectories

- Generalise the 1D results to 3D by treating \(x(t)\), \(y(t)\) and \(z(t)\) independently.
- Derive the boundary‑condition matrices and cost matrices for 3D.
- Implement a function that generates a smooth 3D path through user‑defined waypoints and time allocations; visualise it with Matplotlib.
- Analyse how time allocation per segment affects the trajectory's smoothness and snap cost.

### Day 4 (Thu Mar 12) – Constraints & Alternative Costs

- Incorporate derivative constraints (limits on velocity and acceleration) by adding inequality constraints to the QP.
- Explore alternative objective functions, such as minimizing jerk or acceleration; discuss how the required polynomial order changes.
- Implement a QP that enforces maximum velocity and acceleration bounds; test on a 2‑segment path.

### Day 5 (Fri Mar 13) – Orientation & Yaw Integration

- Extend the polynomial framework to include yaw angle (heading) and its derivative.  Derive the cost of minimizing the 4th derivative of yaw and explain why yaw and position are often optimised separately but coupled through time allocation.
- Implement a minimum‑snap trajectory that includes yaw; simulate a small quadrotor trajectory with orientation.

### Day 6 (Sat Mar 14) – Control Topic: Extended‑State Observer (ESO)

- Read about the **extended state observer (ESO)**: it augments the system state with variables representing lumped uncertainties and disturbances.  The ESO estimates both the plant state and total disturbance for robust control.
- Derive the continuous‑time ESO for the second‑order system \(x'' = u + d\), where \(d\) represents unknown disturbance.
- Show how the observer gain \(L\) can be chosen to place the eigenvalues of the augmented system at \(-\omega_o\).
- Implement the ESO in discrete time and simulate its performance on a sinusoidal disturbance; plot the estimation error.

### Day 7 (Sun Mar 15) – Week 1 Project

Create a **minimum‑snap trajectory generator**:

1. Implement a QP‑based solver for 3D minimum‑snap trajectories with waypoints and timing.
2. Integrate velocity/acceleration constraints and yaw optimisation.

4. Summarise the results in a report and note any numerical issues or parameter‑tuning insights.

Deliver the code and report in your `plan/` folder.  Reflect on how the cost functional and boundary conditions influence the shape of the trajectory.

## Week 2 — Kinodynamic Planning & Distance Fields

This week introduces kinodynamic motion planning and combines it with signed‑distance‑field (SDF) methods.  **Kinodynamic planning** is the problem of driving a robot from an initial state to a goal region while obeying both **kinematic and dynamic constraints**.  It explicitly considers robot dynamics, so the planner outputs trajectories that are feasible with respect to differential equations of motion.  SDFs represent the environment by storing, at each point, the Euclidean distance to the nearest obstacle; they enable efficient distance queries and provide gradients that can push trajectories out of obstacles in optimisation.

### Day 8 (Mon Mar 16) – Kinodynamic Planning Fundamentals

- Read the kinodynamic planning overview by Schmerling & Pavone and summarise the definition: trajectories must be collision‑free and dynamically feasible.
- Derive the kinodynamic planning problem as an optimal control problem subject to dynamics \(\dot{x} = f(x,u)\) and constraints on state and input.
- Implement a **double‑integrator** state propagator: given state \((x,v)\) and control \(u\), integrate \(\dot{x}=v, \dot{v}=u\) forward for a small time step.
- Plot sample reachable trajectories under different constant acceleration inputs; discuss how dynamics restrict motion compared to purely geometric planning.

### Day 9 (Tue Mar 17) – Rapidly‑Exploring Random Trees (RRT) with Dynamics

- Implement a basic **kinodynamic RRT**:
  - Sample random states within a 2D position‑velocity space.
  - For each tree node, sample random control inputs and forward‑propagate using the double‑integrator model.
  - Use a distance metric that accounts for both position and velocity.
- Connect new nodes to the tree only if the forward propagation stays within bounds and does not collide with obstacles.
- Discuss the difference between geometric RRT (which uses straight lines in configuration space) and kinodynamic RRT (which uses forward propagation).

### Day 10 (Wed Mar 18) – Euclidean Signed Distance Fields (ESDF)

- Read about SDFs and note that **Euclidean SDFs (ESDFs)** store the Euclidean distance from each voxel to the nearest obstacle, enabling fast collision checking and gradient computation.
- Implement a 2D ESDF:
  - Create a grid representation of a workspace with obstacles.
  - For each cell, compute the Euclidean distance to the nearest obstacle; use efficient algorithms (e.g. distance transform).
- Visualise the ESDF as a heatmap; verify that the gradient points away from obstacles.
- Discuss why ESDFs are better suited for planning than truncated SDFs for Kinect depth maps.

### Day 11 (Thu Mar 19) – Kinodynamic Planning with SDF Costs

- Combine the ESDF with the kinodynamic RRT:
  - When propagating a motion, evaluate the SDF at intermediate points; penalise trajectories that enter cells with small distance values.
  - Use the SDF gradient to bias sampling towards safer regions.
- Implement a cost function that sums the inverse of the SDF along the path (approximating clearance).  Use this cost to select the lowest‑cost successor.
- Test your planner in a workspace with obstacles; compare it to a planner without SDF guidance.

### Day 12 (Fri Mar 20) – Continuous Collision Detection

- Study the difference between discrete and continuous collision detection.  **Continuous collision detection (CCD)** checks for collisions along the robot's entire trajectory, considering motion between discrete time steps.  CCD is critical for high‑speed robots or thin obstacles that could be missed by discrete checks.
- Derive an analytical CCD test for a line segment moving past a circular obstacle: given start and end points of the robot, compute whether the swept volume intersects the circle.
- Implement this CCD test and integrate it into the kinodynamic planner.
- Show scenarios where discrete collision checks fail but CCD detects a collision.

### Day 13 (Sat Mar 21) – Control Topic: Linear Active Disturbance Rejection Control (LADRC)

- Review the ESO concept and its role in ADRC.
- Design a **linear ADRC** for the second‑order plant \(x'' = b u + d\):
  1. Augment the state with the disturbance.
  2. Design an ESO with observer bandwidth \(\omega_o\).
  3. Derive the control law \(u = (1/b)(v_{\text{ref}} - \hat{d} + k_1 (r - \hat{x}_1) + k_2 (\dot{r} - \hat{x}_2))\), where \(k_1\) and \(k_2\) stabilise the extended plant.
- Implement the LADRC in discrete time; tune \(\omega_o\) and feedback gains; test it on a step input with a sinusoidal disturbance and compare it with a traditional PI controller.

### Day 14 (Sun Mar 22) – Week 2 Project

Create a **kinodynamic RRT* planner with ESDF guidance and CCD**:

1. Implement RRT* with re‑wiring to optimise path cost.
2. Use ESDF‑based clearance cost and continuous collision detection for segment validation.
3. Plan a trajectory for a point‑mass robot with double‑integrator dynamics in a cluttered environment.
4. Compare the planned trajectory with a minimum‑snap trajectory computed in Week 1; discuss differences in cost and feasibility.
5. Document your algorithm, showing how kinodynamic constraints and SDF guidance improve over geometric planners.

## Week 4 — Integration, Control & Gimbal/Motor Topics

The final week applies the methods from Weeks 1–2 to motor/gimbal control and robot planning.  You will integrate planning algorithms with LADRC and hall‑sensor calibration.

### Day 15 (Mon Mar 30) – Gimbal Dynamics & Disturbance Compensation

- Study the dynamics of a gimbal or brushless‑motor system: derive a second‑order model \(J\theta'' = \tau_m - \tau_d\), where \(J\) is inertia, \(\tau_m\) is motor torque and \(\tau_d\) models disturbances such as cogging torque and friction.
- Design an LADRC for this system: choose ESO bandwidth and feedback gains; derive the control law.
- Simulate the controller rejecting a torque disturbance; evaluate tracking error.

### Day 16 (Tue Mar 31) – Hall‑Sensor Calibration & Offset Estimation

- Hall sensors give the rotor position in 60‑degree increments; three sensors placed 120° apart provide six binary patterns.  To run FOC, the controller interpolates these patterns to estimate the absolute electrical angle.
- Study the hall sequence calibration procedure: rotate the motor slowly in open loop, record the hall pattern sequence relative to rotor zero, and determine the 6‑step commutation order.
- Implement a calibration routine that:
  1. Runs the motor at low speed.
  2. Captures hall transitions and their corresponding electrical angles.
  3. Computes the offset between the hall pattern and the stator reference frame.
- Document the calibration data and update your motor‑control software.

### Day 17 (Wed Apr 1) – Gimbal Control Implementation

- Combine the calibrated hall‑sensor angle with LADRC to control the gimbal position and velocity.
- Implement the controller on a microcontroller simulator or Python environment; include modules for angle interpolation, ESO disturbance estimation and control law computation.
- Test the controller on reference trajectories (e.g. sine waves or step inputs); measure steady‑state error and disturbance rejection.

### Day 18 (Thu Apr 2) – Advanced Control: LQR & Trajectory Tracking

- Derive a linear‑quadratic regulator (LQR) for the gimbal dynamics: formulate the cost \(J = \int (q_1 \theta^2 + q_2 \dot{\theta}^2 + r u^2)\, dt\) and solve for feedback gains.
- Compare the LQR with the LADRC from Day 15 in terms of tracking performance and disturbance rejection.
- Implement LQR control in discrete time; test on the same trajectories as LADRC; summarise pros and cons.

### Day 19 (Fri Apr 3) – Integration of Planning & Control

- Integrate the minimum‑snap trajectory generator (Week 1) and kinodynamic planner (Week 2) with the gimbal control system:
  - Use the planner to produce waypoints and time allocations.
  - Use the minimum‑snap generator to smooth the path.
  - Use LADRC or LQR to track the reference angles produced by the trajectory.
- Simulate the full pipeline (planning + smoothing + control) in a 2D environment with obstacles.
- Evaluate performance metrics: trajectory cost, computation time, tracking error, disturbance rejection.

### Day 20 (Sat Apr 4) – Reflection & Complement Updates

- Document any bugs, new observations or insights in the `complement/` folder.  For example, note difficulties in tuning observer bandwidth or QP solver settings.
- Abstract these observations into improvements for the knowledge graph; update topic files such as `ladrc.md` or `polynomial_trajectory.md`.

### Day 21 (Sun Apr 5) – Final Project

Build a **complete motion planning and control demonstration**:

1. Define a 2D workspace with obstacles; specify start and goal states (position and velocity).
2. Use your kinodynamic RRT* with SDF guidance and CCD to plan a dynamically feasible path.
3. Use your minimum‑snap solver to smooth the path into a polynomial trajectory.
4. Implement LADRC (or LQR) to track the trajectory on a simulated gimbal/robot model with hall‑sensor feedback.
5. Present the results: trajectory plots, control effort, disturbance rejection and collision avoidance.
6. Write a detailed report explaining the design choices, mathematical derivations and experimental results.  Suggest directions for improving the planner (e.g. using higher‑order dynamics or considering time‑optimality).
